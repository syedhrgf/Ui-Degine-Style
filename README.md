

# Core UI Styles

### 1. Neumorphism

* Soft, extruded look using light and shadow
* Creates “pressed” or “raised” elements
* Can reduce contrast → be careful with accessibility

---

### 2. Glassmorphism

* Frosted glass effect with transparency + blur
* Modern and visually appealing
* Best used for overlays, cards, modals

---

### 3. Flat Design

* No shadows, minimal styling
* Clean, fast, and highly scalable
* Widely used in modern apps

---

### 4. Material Design

* Uses shadows, depth, and motion
* Structured and consistent design system
* Great for scalable apps and teams

---

### 5. Skeuomorphism

* Mimics real-world objects
* Mostly outdated but still used in niche cases

---

### 6. Minimalism

* Focus on simplicity and whitespace
* Reduces clutter, improves readability

---

### 7. Dark Mode UI

* Dark backgrounds with light text
* Reduces eye strain, saves battery

---

### 8. Brutalism

* Bold, raw, unconventional layouts
* Used for creative or experimental designs

---

### 9. Claymorphism

* Soft 3D elements with vibrant colors
* More playful version of Neumorphism

---

### 10. Retro / Vintage

* Old-school pixel or classic design styles
* Used for nostalgic or gaming interfaces

---

### 11. Cyberpunk / Futuristic

* Neon colors, glowing elements
* Sci-fi inspired UI

---

## Recommended Usage

### Best for Production Apps

* Flat Design
* Material Design
* Minimalism
* Dark Mode

---

### Use Carefully

* Neumorphism
* Glassmorphism
* Claymorphism

(Reason: Can affect usability and accessibility)

---

###  Best for Creative / Branding

* Brutalism
* Retro
* Cyberpunk

---

## Guidelines

* Prioritize **usability over aesthetics**
* Maintain **consistent design system**
* Ensure **accessibility (contrast, readability)**
* Optimize for **performance (avoid heavy effects)**
* Combine styles carefully (don’t mix too many)


## Conclusion

There is no “best” UI style — the right choice depends on:

* Product type
* Target users
* Performance needs
